DIY Guides

What you can fix
yourself — safely.

Aria tells you the truth: some HVAC tasks are genuinely homeowner-friendly. Others will void your warranty or hurt you. Know the difference.

Easy Change Your Air Filter Save $50–$100/yr + Expand

The single highest-impact DIY HVAC task. A clogged filter forces your system to work harder, raises energy bills, and is the hidden cause of dozens of expensive repairs. Do this every 30–90 days.

  1. Turn off your HVAC system at the thermostat.
  2. Find your filter — usually in the return air vent or inside the air handler. Check both if unsure.
  3. Note the size printed on the frame (e.g. 20x25x1) and the direction of the airflow arrow.
  4. Remove the old filter. Hold it up to light — if you can't see through it, it was overdue.
  5. Insert the new filter with the arrow pointing toward the air handler (the direction air flows in).
  6. Turn system back on. Done.
⚠ Cheap fiberglass filters let more particles through. MERV 8–11 is the sweet spot for most LKN homes — high enough to protect the system, not so restrictive it starves airflow.
Easy Flush the Condensate Drain Line Save $75–$200 + Expand

In Lake Norman's humid summers, drain line clogs are the #1 HVAC service call. The good news: they're almost entirely preventable with a $2 bottle of white vinegar.

  1. Locate the drain line — it's the white PVC pipe near your indoor air handler, usually 3/4" diameter.
  2. Find the access port (a T-shaped cap) on the drain line near the air handler.
  3. Pour about 1/4 cup of distilled white vinegar into the access port.
  4. Wait 30 minutes. The vinegar kills algae and mold that cause clogs.
  5. Flush with a cup of water to clear the line.
  6. Do this once a month in summer, every 2–3 months otherwise.
⚠ If your drain pan is overflowing or your system is shutting itself off, the clog may be beyond the access point. At that stage — call a pro. A wet-vac from the outdoor drain end can help, but line damage is possible if you're not careful.
Easy Reset a Tripped Circuit Breaker Save $75–$150 + Expand

If your AC suddenly stops running entirely, a tripped breaker is the most common — and most overlooked — cause. Before you call a tech, check this first.

  1. Go to your electrical panel (breaker box — usually in the garage or utility room).
  2. Look for a breaker labeled AC, HVAC, Air Handler, or Condenser that's in the middle "tripped" position.
  3. Push the breaker fully to OFF first, then back to ON. Don't just flip it from middle to on.
  4. Wait 5 minutes, then try the AC. It needs time to reset pressure.
  5. If it trips again immediately — stop. Something is drawing too much current. Call a tech.
⚠ A breaker that trips repeatedly is telling you something — compressor struggling, refrigerant issue, or wiring fault. Do not keep resetting it. That's how fires start.
Medium Install a Smart Thermostat Save $150–$300/yr + Expand

A smart thermostat (Nest, Ecobee, Honeywell T6) can cut HVAC energy use by 10–15%. Most are homeowner-installable in under an hour if your wiring is standard. Lake Norman homes built after 2000 almost always qualify.

  1. Turn off power to your HVAC at the breaker before touching any wires.
  2. Remove the old thermostat and photograph the wires before disconnecting anything.
  3. Check your wires: you need at least R, G, Y, C (common) wires. If no C-wire, use the adapter that comes with most smart thermostats.
  4. Mount the new baseplate and connect wires to matching terminals.
  5. Attach the display, restore power, and follow the thermostat's setup wizard.
  6. Test heating and cooling modes before closing everything up.
⚠ Heat pumps have different wiring (O/B wire for reversing valve). If you have a heat pump, make sure your thermostat is heat-pump compatible and follow its specific wiring diagram.
Call a Pro Refrigerant Recharge Legal reason — not DIY + Why

Refrigerant handling requires EPA 608 certification — it's illegal for uncertified individuals to purchase or handle refrigerants like R-410A or R-22. Beyond the legal issue, refrigerant work requires specialized equipment (manifold gauges, recovery machines) and the ability to diagnose why levels are low in the first place.

Low refrigerant almost always means a leak — and recharging without finding the leak just delays the real problem.

⚠ If someone offers to recharge your refrigerant cheaply without checking for leaks first — walk away. You'll pay again in 6 months. Get the leak found and fixed, then recharge.
